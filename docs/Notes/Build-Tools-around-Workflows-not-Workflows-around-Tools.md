## About

---

> *"besides email and calendar, I live within a system of my own tools, and it works well for me."* <sup>1</sup>

Linus mentions how he has been replacing all his digital tools by the ones he creates, deploys, and maintains. From knowledge management, to even creating his own post-it notes app, everything he uses was made for him, and his workflow.

* Every person has their own way of thinking, remembering, and processing information.
* They tend to develop different habits, and workflows for their tasks.
* That's the reason why different people use different tools - based on which one mirrors their workflows / habits / thinking pattern.
* The tools need to be built around their specific workflows, not the other way around.
* Good tools fit perfectly around our workflows, bad tools don’t.<sup>1</sup>

> *the tools can truly be an extension of the way my brain thinks and organizes information about the world around me.* <sup>1</sup>

I do resonate with this. When I was setting up this note-taking workflow for myself, I had to make a lot of choices. Where to compromise and adapt to the tool's workflow, and where I cannot compromise. More on this ~~later~~ now.

> I like Obsidian for note taking. I tried Notion, OneNote, Evernote, etc. I think Obsidian has two advantages. 
> 1. It can be used completely offline.
> 2. It stores data as markdown files. Which can be extended via HTML.
> These make it great for publishing as a Wiki.
> But, I had to customize some parts of it, so I can self-publish my notes.
> mkdocs-material also has some restrictions, I have to adapt my workflow to fit into the mkdocs structure.>
